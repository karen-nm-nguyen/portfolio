Travelers are frustrated by planning itineraries from sources that aren’t always reliable, leaving them many missed opportunities to receive the full experience of their travel destination.

01. user research

Our collective goal was to understand the overall travel experience and find underlying pain points and obstacles. We wanted to know “What is an avid traveler’s roundtrip experience?” We had sent out a survey to 30 people and conducted one-on-one user interviews with five individuals to gather data.

our findings:

Starting with basic demographics, the data collection showed the majority gender surveyed was 58.6% female. With the majority age range between 25-30. Traveling 3-5 trips annually. In terms of travel logistics, it was seen that taking the plane and a car/ride-share was favored over the weekend.

Daily itineraries and activities were primarily driven by the traveler’s available time and proximity of the venue/activity to the place of stay. Half of the surveyed group found their recommendation sources reliable.

affinity diagram:

Our Affinity Diagram yielded common insights among those surveyed by grouping like responses into various categories. These included travel frequency, duration, attitudes, etc.



user persona:

Lastly, our Empathy Map led us to identifying our User Persona: someone who loves to experience the local life.Her daily philosophy is to “go-with-the-flow” as she travels somewhat loosely with a general itinerary. However, she is one for accurate and credible reviews since she asks for recommendations on what to do. Being a go-getter, she wants to advance her career and stay healthy, but remembers to have fun in the social crowd while doing so.

user insight:

Ambitious and free-spirited Jeni needs to experience more social events because her social anxiety grows when she feels she’s been disconnected from the world for too long.

problem statement:

Experiencing the best of local food and culture are top priorities when traveling, but it often goes unfulfilled because of the lack of reliable resources. If this continues, the magic of exclusive knowledge and adventure will be inaccessible when traveling. Therefore, we believe that Waylien’s all-in-one service platform is able to bring people together. Our app puts the power of choice in the tourist’s hands to select their guides, but leaves the knowledge to the tour guides themselves.

feature prioritization matrix:

According to the matrix, ideas regarding safety and personalization fall into the High Priority, High Impact quadrant. Features that would be an extra bonus, such as specific preference in itinerary, recommended lodging, and split fees for group tours fall into the Low Priority, Low Impact quadrant.

value proposition:

“Waylien shows you a way to experience trips “like the locals do.” Select the ultimate friendly local to accompany you to hidden gems, best dining, and extravagant shopping. Your fun day awaits.”

user journey map:

I created a User Journey Map to identify segments in the user’s journey/scenario. Within my findings, I discovered that there was friction and a change of emotions within the Planning and Reaction stage.

competitive analysis:

My direct competitor was WithLocals and my indirect competitor was TripScout. WithLocals offered personal tour guides, whereas TripScout lets you be your own guide with its itinerary organization feature.

digital high-fidelity:

Before I went to test my prototype, I went back to refine my color palette and type choices to really portray consistency throughout my screens and showcase the “young and fresh” feel of Waylien. I kept the color yellow to indicate friendship and friendliness, but added a gradient to soften the bold color. Swapping out the blue for pink also elevated the modern feel.

usability testing:

iteration:

A huge difference was developing more of the Profile part of the app where users can see and edit upcoming trips individually as well as edit their Settings information. A split between “Settings” and “Profile.” Settings lives within Profile, but Profile allows the user to access different trips and to edit specific budgets and activities per trip.

final thoughts

This was an amazing project to work on! I appreciate a lot of the hands on work to create this.

Some key takeaways:

Iteration is necessary – I always thought that revising something focuses a lot on how I failed first and foremost, but I found that it’s necessary to fail (or not get it quite on the dot) to get closer to the goal.

Testing is a valuable joy – on the same note as iteration, it’s difficult to see your users fail at a task, but it also brought out some of the best ideas that are overlooked. I felt like I was so enveloped into my work that I glanced over a lot of features and distinctions that I didn’t even think about. The feedback I received from testing had me reiterate certain screens that ended up becoming the best screens in terms of flow and design.
